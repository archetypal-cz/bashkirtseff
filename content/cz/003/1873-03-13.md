Dřív, když bylo hezky, všechno bylo tak pravidelné. Hodinu jsem jezdila v kočáře, hodinu chodila pěšky. Viděla jsem všechny lidi, toalety, pozorovala jsem, pozorovali mě. Nejvíc mám ráda společnost lady Howardové, protože tam jednou byl vévoda, můj [Přeškrtnuto: milovaný, můj drahý]. Kde je teď? Jak bych to ráda věděla!
Nesmím myslet na Boreela, abych si zasloužila Hamiltona. Nezasloužím si ho, když si budu vymýšlet nějaké Boreely. Chci-li tak velké štěstí, tak mimořádné štěstí - být vévodovou ženou - nesmím myslet na nic jiného. Kdyby šlo o něco obyčejného, mohla bych si to dovolit. Ale když toužím po něm? Ne. On není jako ostatní. Chci-li si zasloužit štěstí, po kterém toužím, musím myslet jen na to.

%% 003.0477 %%
%% WEATHER AS SOCIAL DETERMINANT: Marie's distress about rain is not melodrama but practical reality. The Promenade des Anglais and Jardin Public concerts were the primary venues for the daily social ritual of "voir et etre vue" (see and be seen). Without fine weather, there was no promenade, no display of toilettes, no chance encounters with eligible men. The entire social economy of Nice's winter season depended on outdoor performance. When it rained, society scattered - some to Monaco's indoor casino attractions, others staying home entirely.
MONACO AS ALTERNATIVE: When Nice weather failed, Monaco (30km east) offered the casino and its social scene as an alternative. The suggestion that "le monde" goes to Monaco in bad weather indicates how integrated the two locations were in the Riviera social circuit. The Duke of Hamilton, as a racing commissioner and pigeon shooting patron, would have been a regular Monaco visitor.
"MERITER" THE DUKE - RELIGIOUS AND ROMANTIC: Marie's language of "deserving" Hamilton reveals her fusion of religious thinking with romantic ambition. She constructs a quasi-devotional bargain with God: if she sacrifices lesser romantic attachments (Boreel), she will "merit" the greater prize (the Duke). This reflects both her Orthodox upbringing and the transactional nature of 19th-century marriage markets, where one's worthiness for a match was measured in moral as well as material terms.
THE "BATACLAN" - MARIE'S DISMISSIVE INTIMACY: Her use of "bataclan" (roughly: entourage, crowd, mob) to describe both men and women of society shows both sophistication and contempt. She loves watching them, feels bereft without them, yet dismisses them as a circus. Lady Howard's "bataclan" is precious only because the Duke was once among it.
SELF-CENSORSHIP AND LATER CRITIQUE: Marie's crossed-out words ("mon amour, mon cheri") and marginal notes ("Imbecile. Oh quelle sotte!") document her practice of self-editing. The later Marie is embarrassed by the younger Marie's effusions, yet preserves them - a pattern throughout the diary that makes it both primary source and retrospective commentary. %%
