1873-02-12

  1. The Russian New Year was celebrated on January 13th according to the Gregorian calendar used in Western Europe, due to Russia’s use of the Julian calendar at that time. ↩

  2. The Floréal balls were significant social events in Nice during the winter season, attended by both local aristocracy and wealthy visitors. ↩

  3. The Cercle Masséna was named after André Masséna, one of Napoleon’s marshals who was born in Nice. It was an important social institution where the local aristocracy and wealthy visitors gathered for balls, concerts, and other cultural events. ↩

  4. Mlle Collignon was Marie’s English governess who played a significant role in her education and social development. Her presence in these entries reflects the typical arrangement for young ladies of Marie’s social class, who were educated by private governesses. ↩

  5. The public gardens (“jardins publics”) were a central feature of Nice’s social life, where the wealthy would promenade to see and be seen, even in inclement weather. ↩

  6. Marie Bashkirtseff was born on November 11, 1858. The January 24 date she mentions here was likely her name day celebration according to the Russian Orthodox calendar, which was common practice among Russian nobility. ↩

  7. This passage shows remarkable self-awareness for a thirteen-year-old, suggesting either precocious maturity or later editing of her diary entries. The Duke of Hamilton was indeed known for his sporting interests and social life. ↩

  8. The Wittgenstein mentioned here was likely a member of the prominent European aristocratic family, showing the social circles in which the Duke moved. ↩

  9. Horse racing was a major social event on the French Riviera during the winter season, attracting wealthy British visitors like the Duke of Hamilton. ↩

  10. Marie’s English lessons with Mlle Collignon had begun on January 12, 1872, as noted in a previous entry. This consistent study would later enable her to write parts of her diary in English. ↩

  11. Marie’s strategic analysis of romantic relationships was unusually sophisticated for a thirteen-year-old, suggesting either remarkable precocity or later editing of her diary entries. ↩

  12. This 1884 annotation (“Stupidité, ingénuité”) was added by Marie herself eleven years later, when she was reviewing her earlier diaries. Such later comments provide valuable insight into how she viewed her younger self’s thoughts and feelings. ↩

  13. The races were major social events in Nice’s winter season, providing opportunities for the local society to gather and display their status. For Marie, they represented a chance to see the Duke in a public setting where her presence would be socially acceptable. ↩

  14. The Howards and Prince Gagarine were prominent figures in Nice’s international society. Marie’s casual betting of bonbons shows both her youth and her participation in adult social customs. ↩

  15. The position of race commissioner was a prestigious role typically held by prominent members of society, highlighting the Duke’s social status. ↩

  16. Marie’s diaries did indeed become important literary documents, though valued more for their psychological insight and social history than as stylistic models. Her self-awareness about her early literary pretensions shows remarkable maturity. ↩

  17. The pigeon shooting competitions in Monaco were prestigious events that attracted international aristocracy. Being “mis hors concours” meant the Duke was excluded from the competition, possibly due to his previous victories or his status as a patron. ↩

  18. The Cercle de la Méditerranée was one of Nice’s most prestigious social venues. The Vicomtesse Vigier, who hosted this performance, was the former opera singer Sophie Cruvelli, adding another layer of significance to the musical evening. ↩

  19. Bellini’s “La Sonnambula” (The Sleepwalker) was a popular romantic opera, its themes of love, misunderstanding, and eventual recognition perhaps resonating with Marie’s own romantic hopes. ↩

  20. The reference to Baden (“Bade”) indicates that Marie had seen the Duke before her time in Nice, establishing a longer history to her fascination with him. ↩

  21. Marie’s ability to simultaneously experience intense emotions and analyze them with some detachment is remarkable for a thirteen-year-old, though this sophistication has led some scholars to question whether she later edited these early diary entries. ↩

  22. Marie’s ability to acknowledge Gioia’s superior beauty, despite her jealousy, demonstrates a level of honesty in her diary-keeping that would later make these journals valuable historical documents. ↩

  23. The mention of Baron Bach illustrates how Russian aristocratic families sought suitable marriages for their children while wintering on the French Riviera. ↩

  24. The contrast between the death of Madame U. and Marie’s preoccupation with missing the Duke illustrates the sometimes jarring juxtaposition of serious social events with adolescent concerns in her diary. ↩

  25. The regatta was another significant social event in Nice’s winter season, attracting international visitors and providing opportunities for social display. ↩

  26. The presence of a “jolie Ecossaise” (pretty Scottish girl) at the ball indicates the international character of Nice’s winter society, which attracted wealthy families from across Europe. ↩

  27. Marie’s observation about people criticizing those they secretly admire shows psychological insight that seems beyond her years, though it may reflect later editing or naturally precocious understanding of human nature. ↩

  28. The comparison of the Duke to a butcher by Hélène provides a rare glimpse of how others viewed him, contrasting sharply with Marie’s idealized perception. ↩

  29. Marie’s language studies with Mlle Collignon (English) and Micaletti (Italian) reflect both her privileged education and her cosmopolitan upbringing. Her satisfaction with her progress suggests she took her studies seriously despite her romantic preoccupations. ↩
