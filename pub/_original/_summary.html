1873-02

February 1873 - Monthly Summary

Overview

February 1873 marks the beginning of Marie Bashkirtseff’s second notebook (Carnet N° 2). During this month, Marie is living in Nice with her family, participating in the winter social season that attracted wealthy European and Russian aristocrats to the French Riviera. The month coincides with the carnival season, culminating in Mardi Gras on February 25.

Key Themes

Romantic Preoccupations

Marie’s diary is dominated by her romantic interests, primarily:

  1. The Duke of Hamilton - Marie’s primary obsession, whom she rarely sees but intensely admires from afar. She considers him the perfect match due to his title, wealth, and aristocratic status. She is jealous of his relationship with a woman named Gioia.

  2. Alfred Boreel - A handsome young man Marie sees frequently on the promenade. Her feelings for him fluctuate throughout the month, sometimes claiming to love him, other times dismissing him as merely a distraction. She is disappointed to learn he is not a baron as she initially believed.

Social Activities

Marie’s days revolve around: - Daily promenades (on foot, in carriages, or occasionally on horseback) - Music performances in public gardens - Theater and opera attendance - Carnival festivities - Balls and soirées - Church attendance - Family’s “reception day” when they receive visitors

Carnival Season

The Nice Carnival features prominently in the latter part of the month, with Marie describing: - Decorated floats (“chars”) - Costumed cavalcades - Participants in white dominos (cloaks) with iron masks - Exchange of flowers and confetti - Electric illuminations of the streets

Self-Image and Appearance

Marie is highly conscious of her appearance and how others perceive her: - She frequently describes her outfits in detail - Notes when she blushes in the presence of her romantic interests - Examines herself in mirrors and evaluates her attractiveness - Strategizes about how to appear indifferent to increase others’ interest

Class Consciousness

Marie’s aristocratic values are evident throughout: - She evaluates potential romantic partners based on titles and wealth - Expresses disappointment when learning Boreel is not titled - Distinguishes between “society” people and others - Concerns herself with proper introductions and social protocols

Key People

  • Duke of Hamilton - Aristocrat who is the object of Marie’s primary infatuation
  • Alfred Boreel - Handsome young man who initially interests Marie
  • Gioia - Woman associated with the Duke of Hamilton, object of Marie’s jealousy
  • Mlle Collignon - Marie’s teacher
  • Maman (Marie’s mother) - Participates in social activities with Marie
  • Dina - Likely a relative or close family friend
  • Sophie - Another female companion
  • Count Gabrielli - A social acquaintance
  • The Howard family - Social connections with children Marie interacts with

Key Places

  • Promenade des Anglais - Main seaside promenade in Nice where society gathered
  • Villa Acqua Viva - Marie’s residence in Nice (No. 51 Promenade des Anglais)
  • The French Theater - Where Marie attends performances
  • The Italian Theater/Opera - Another venue for performances
  • Monaco - Nearby principality with casino, viewed as a place of vice and corruption
  • Cercle Masséna - Social club hosting balls and matinées

Marie’s Inner Life

Marie’s diary reveals a young woman with: - Intense romantic fantasies - Social ambitions - Strategic thinking about relationships - Self-awareness about her appearance and effect on others - Dramatic emotional swings - Religious framework for understanding her desires (frequent prayers to God) - Concern with propriety alongside romantic obsessions - Tendency toward self-criticism (evident in her later marginal notes)

Historical Context

February 1873 captures the winter social season in Nice, when wealthy Europeans and Russians would escape the northern winter for the mild Mediterranean climate. The carnival season was a major social event, with elaborate festivities leading up to Mardi Gras. Marie’s diary provides a window into the social life, values, and preoccupations of a young aristocratic woman in this setting.

1873-03

March 1873 - Monthly Summary

Overview

The March 1873 entries (1st-12th) conclude Marie Bashkirtseff’s second notebook (Carnet N° 2). This period marks a significant transition in Marie’s life as she learns that the Duke of Hamilton has departed Nice, forcing her to reconsider her romantic aspirations. The month also features a pivotal moment when Marie discovers her singing talent might provide a path to fame and the Duke’s attention.

Key Themes

The Departure of the Duke

Early in March, Marie learns that the Duke of Hamilton’s yacht has left Nice eight days earlier, meaning he has departed without her ever having been properly introduced to him. This news devastates her: - She expresses rage and despair at being “ignored” by him - Laments having no purpose for her promenades and social appearances - Imagines him in Paris, planning to somehow see him there - Compares her feelings to those for someone who has died

Singing as a Path to Glory

A transformative moment occurs on March 9 when Marie sings for the Howard children and receives enthusiastic praise. This experience leads to an epiphany: - She realizes her voice could make her a celebrated singer (cantatrice) - Envisions a career that would bring her fame, admiration, and social power - Believes this path could ultimately win the Duke’s attention and love - Sees this as divinely inspired - God showing her how to achieve her desires - Reconciles her aristocratic status with a performing career by emphasizing her noble birth

Continued Ambivalence Toward Boreel

Marie’s feelings about Boreel remain complicated: - She criticizes him for imitating the Duke’s lifestyle without having the means - Expresses disappointment at his visits to Monaco and rumors of his behavior there - Admits to missing seeing him on the promenade - Notes with satisfaction when he looks at her admiringly - Strategizes about appearing indifferent to increase his interest

Family Crisis

On March 11, Marie’s mother suffers what appears to be a nervous attack: - The family fears for her life momentarily - Marie describes this as “the strongest emotion I have ever experienced” - Multiple doctors are called - By the following day, her mother is improving but still bedridden - The incident reveals Marie’s genuine attachment to her mother despite her self-absorption

Social Activities

Marie continues her regular social routine: - Daily promenades (on foot, in carriages, or occasionally on horseback) - Theater and opera attendance (including Verdi’s “La Forza del Destino” and “Lucia di Lammermoor”) - Family’s Monday reception day - Visits to and from acquaintances - Photography session at Shier’s studio

Key People

  • Duke of Hamilton - The primary object of Marie’s romantic obsession, who has now left Nice
  • Alfred Boreel - Young man who continues to interest Marie despite her criticisms
  • Gioia - Woman associated with the Duke, presumed to have left Nice with him
  • The Howard family - Social connections whose children’s admiration of Marie’s singing inspires her career ambitions
  • Mlle Collignon - Marie’s teacher, who earns praise for her helpfulness during the mother’s health crisis
  • Maman (Marie’s mother) - Suffers a health crisis that briefly shifts Marie’s focus from romantic concerns
  • Count Gabrielli - Social acquaintance who inadvertently delivers the news of the Duke’s departure

Key Places

  • Paris - Where Marie believes the Duke has gone, and where she hopes to see him at the Bois de Boulogne
  • Monaco - Described as a “cursed place” and “awful abyss” that corrupts young men like Boreel
  • The Italian and French Theaters - Venues for performances Marie attends
  • The Howard residence - Where Marie’s singing talent is recognized and celebrated

Marie’s Inner Life

The March entries reveal significant aspects of Marie’s psychology: - Her tendency to dramatize emotions while simultaneously analyzing them - Her strategic thinking about social and romantic situations - Her ability to quickly pivot from disappointment to new ambitions - Her religious framework for understanding events and desires - Her self-awareness about her appearance and effect on others - Her class consciousness and social ambitions - Her later self-criticism (evident in marginal notes added in 1874)

Significant Developments

  1. The Duke’s Departure - Forces Marie to confront the unlikelihood of her romantic fantasy
  2. Discovery of Vocal Talent - Provides a new direction for her ambitions
  3. Mother’s Health Crisis - Momentarily shifts Marie’s focus from romantic concerns to family
  4. Career Ambitions - Marie begins to envision herself as a celebrated singer while maintaining her social status

Historical Context

These entries provide valuable insights into: - The conclusion of the winter social season in Nice - The importance of proper introductions and social protocols in aristocratic circles - The tension between aristocratic values and artistic careers for women - The perception of Monaco as a place of moral corruption - The cultural significance of opera and theater in 1870s European society

The Second Half of March (13-31)

Beginning of Carnet N° 3

March 13 marks the beginning of Marie’s third notebook, with her own annotation from 1874 calling the end of the previous book “stupide.” The new notebook continues with intensified romantic obsessions and social observations.

Definitive Break with Boreel

Marie’s feelings toward Boreel undergo a complete transformation: - March 16: Admits she still feels “something” but only “remnants of past love” - March 25: Declares him “completely indifferent before God” - March 27: States definitively that Boreel is “ugly with a tired face” - “it’s over for you!” - Employs strategy of ignoring him to pique his interest

Continued Hope for Hamilton Despite His Absence

  • March 14: Sees Duke’s carriage heading to Gioia’s, sparking hope he’s returned
  • March 22: Discovers in Swiss Times that Duke is at Hotel du Louvre in Naples
  • March 26: Father describes Duke as “not stupid, a bit stiff, slightly haughty, sometimes very nice and cheerful”
  • March 27: Learns Duke is commissioner for the pigeon shooting competition
  • March 30: Has elaborate dream of Duke visiting their home and talking about her

Observations of Gioia

Marie’s jealousy and fascination with the Duke’s mistress intensifies: - March 19: Sees Gioia in black with an expression of complete satisfaction - March 22: Observes her elegant surroundings and perfect grooming - March 24: Learns from Bensa that Audiffret gave Gioia a 20,000 franc gift - March 29: Notes Gioia “wasn’t pretty that day” - March 31: Sees Gioia in oriental fabric, notes “she always looks at me”

Conflict with Mlle Collignon

  • March 18: Dispute about salons leads to tension
  • March 24: Collignon writes letter complaining about Marie’s “impertinent and vain character”
  • Marie anticipates her departure with mixed feelings

Social Strategies and Plans

  • March 31: Develops plan to befriend Souvoroff and Galve families to get closer to Duke
  • Admires Mlle de Galve’s elegant style, especially her grey felt hat
  • Hope that purchasing a villa will improve their social connections

Wardrobe Crisis

Marie increasingly struggles with inadequate clothing: - Wears borrowed and “antediluvian” dresses - Desperately wants proper attire for the pigeon shooting - April 1: Combines pieces from different outfits to create ensembles

Family Concerns

  • Mother’s health improves after crisis
  • Family discusses summer trip to Russia, which terrifies Marie
  • Reflects on brother Paul’s future and plans to guide him

Religious Vows and Bargains

  • March 21: Treats arithmetic lesson as test - if completed without help, she’ll become Duchess
  • March 25 & 29: Repeatedly vows to God not to strain her voice
  • Maintains faith that God will somehow unite her with the Duke despite distance

Key Dates in Late March

March 13 - Beginning of Carnet N° 3; promises God to stop thinking about Boreel

March 22 - Discovers Duke is in Naples through Swiss Times

March 24 - Collignon’s complaint letter; learns of Gioia’s arrangements

March 27 - Duke confirmed as Tir commissioner; views stereoscope photos

March 30 - Dreams of Duke visiting and mentioning Berthe

March 31 - Plans to befriend elite families; hopes villa purchase will help

Conclusion

The complete March 1873 entries show Marie navigating between romantic despair and determined hope. While the Duke’s absence forces her to confront reality, she maintains faith in divine intervention and develops increasingly elaborate strategies to achieve her goals. The month ends with Marie preparing for the April pigeon shooting competition, her last hope for the season.

1873-04

April 1873 Summary

Key Events and Themes

The Duke of Hamilton’s Absence

  • Marie continues to pine for the Duke who remains absent throughout April
  • She has vivid visions of him during Good Friday church service (April 18)
  • Dreams of meeting him at the Vienna World Exhibition
  • Discovers Gioia has left for Russia regarding inheritance matters, which gives her hope

Family Conflicts

  • Ongoing issues with governess Mlle Collignon’s teaching methods and neglect of lessons
  • Major confrontation with the Tolstoy family (April 24) who attempt to enter their home after 8 months of feuding
  • Mother falls ill after the Tolstoy incident, has an attack (April 27)

Religious Observance

  • Orthodox Holy Week (April 14-20) with daily church attendance
  • Confession on April 19 where priest calls her vain
  • Easter midnight mass and celebrations
  • Marie struggles to pray properly in church, finds it easier at home alone

Social Life and Appearance

  • Constant wardrobe anxieties - winter clothes too heavy, no suitable spring clothes
  • Orders new dresses from Paris and copies Mlle de Galve’s Italian straw hat style
  • Attends pigeon shooting competitions at Monaco, discovers genuine love for the sport
  • Sees Lord Carlos Hamilton (the Duke’s brother) multiple times

Romantic Developments

  • Boreel fades completely from her affections
  • Mother begins pushing Gritsia Miloradovitch as a marriage prospect
  • Dramatic scenes when Mlle Collignon spots her former lover with another woman (May 1)

Literary and Philosophical Moments

  • Reads Mrs. Henry Wood’s novel about working persistently toward goals with God’s blessing
  • Continues intense religious faith and prayers for the Duke
  • Strange shared dreams with others about drowning and world ending

Notable Quotes

“Il était avec moi, d’ailleurs il ne me quitte jamais, il est toujours devant mes yeux.” (April 27)

“C’est pour la première fois que j’aime véritablement!” (April 18)

“Je dois être ou la duchesse de Hamilton… ou recherchée, connue, estimée, courue après, spoken of, vivre au milieu des fêtes de la plus haute société” (April 25)

Health and Well-being

  • Marie rises at 5:30 AM, in bed by 10 PM
  • Takes sea baths
  • Increasingly frustrated with Nice and desperate to leave for proper education in Florence, Rome, etc.
  • Family trapped in Nice by ongoing Russian lawsuit

Social Observations

  • Nice society declining as season ends
  • Princess Souvoroff’s satisfied air comes from wealth and getting everything she desires
  • Audiffret now “cock of the village” but being ruined by Gioia

Future Plans

  • Hopes to attend Vienna World Exhibition
  • Considers moves to Italy
  • Dreams of life in high society or as stage celebrity (prefers the former)

1873-08

August 1873: Paris and Return to Nice

Overview

August 1873 marks a significant period in Marie Bashkirtseff’s diary, documenting her visit to Paris and subsequent return to Nice. The month begins with Marie in Paris, where she engages in social activities, shopping, and cultural pursuits while harboring hopes of encountering the Duke of Hamilton. The latter half of the month chronicles her journey back to Nice and her reintegration into family life there, where her infatuation with the Duke becomes a topic of family discussion and teasing.

Main Topics and Themes

Paris Visit

  • Social Activities: Marie attends various social gatherings, visits the Bois de Boulogne, and dines at fashionable establishments
  • Shopping and Fashion: Extensive descriptions of visits to Worth and other fashion houses, ordering dresses and accessories
  • Cultural Pursuits: Visits to the Louvre and other cultural sites, reflections on art and beauty
  • Unrequited Love: Continued obsession with the Duke of Hamilton, disappointment at not seeing him in Paris

Journey to Nice

  • Travel Observations: Detailed account of the train journey from Paris to Nice, with stops in Mâcon, Marseille, and Toulon
  • Self-Reflection: Contemplative moments during travel, considering her future and ambitions
  • Reluctant Return: Expressions of disdain for Nice (“Paris que j’adore et Nice que j’abhorre”)

Family Dynamics in Nice

  • Family Teasing: Marie’s infatuation with the Duke becomes public knowledge within her family, leading to teasing
  • Mother’s Opposition: Her mother’s skepticism about the Duke and preference for a Russian match
  • Social Status Concerns: Discussions about the Duke’s wealth and Marie’s marriage prospects

Key Events

  1. August 11-20: Marie’s time in Paris, filled with shopping, social visits, and cultural activities
  2. August 21-28: Marie’s journey from Paris back to Nice, with various stops along the way
  3. August 25-26: Marie’s emotional reaction to seeing Gioia’s portrait at the Walery photography studio
  4. August 29: News of Abramovitch’s death, which deeply affects Marie
  5. August 30-31: Family discussions about Marie’s marriage prospects, with focus on the Duke of Hamilton

People Mentioned

  • Duke of Hamilton: The object of Marie’s romantic obsession, discussed extensively in family conversations
  • Gioia: A beautiful woman in Nice who Marie perceives as a rival, whose portrait Marie sees in Paris
  • Abramovitch: A friend or acquaintance whose sudden death is reported on August 29
  • Princess Galitzine: A family friend who encourages Marie’s fantasies about the Duke, calling her “duchesse”
  • Walitsky: A family acquaintance who teases Marie with stories about the Duke’s eccentric behavior
  • Worth: The famous fashion designer who Marie visits to order dresses
  • Various family members: Mother, aunt, Dina (likely a cousin), and others who participate in discussions about Marie’s future

Social and Cultural Context

  • Fashion and Appearance: Marie’s detailed descriptions of clothing, accessories, and beauty routines reflect the importance of appearance in upper-class society
  • Marriage Prospects: Discussions about potential husbands reveal the economic and social considerations that governed aristocratic marriages
  • Travel in the 1870s: Marie’s journey from Paris to Nice provides insights into train travel during this period
  • Aristocratic Leisure: Activities like promenades, visits to the Bois de Boulogne, and dining out illustrate typical pastimes of the wealthy

Marie’s Personal Development

  • Self-Image: Continued preoccupation with her appearance and how others perceive her
  • Romantic Idealism: Elaborate fantasies about the Duke of Hamilton, despite having no actual relationship with him
  • Intellectual Ambitions: Plans to resume studies in Nice with an ambitious schedule of nine hours of lessons per day
  • Philosophical Reflections: Surprisingly mature reflections on love, jealousy, and marriage in the August 31 entry

Setting Details

  • Paris: Descriptions of fashionable areas, shops, and cultural venues in the French capital
  • Train Journey: Observations of Mâcon, Marseille, Toulon, and other stops on the route from Paris to Nice
  • Nice: Marie’s return to her family home, with mentions of local landmarks like Monte-Carlo and the Var

Significant Quotes

  • “Tous les endroits sont égaux pour moi excepté Paris que j’adore et Nice que j’abhorre.” (August 28)
  • “Ah ! comme je voudrais être sa femme.” (August 31)
  • “Je ne puis épouser un homme pauvre, c’est contre ma nature, ce serait le malheur des deux.” (August 31)
  • “Je méprise la jalousie. Si un homme qui m’aime pense à une autre femme, ça veut dire qu’il ne m’aime plus, et il est ridicule d’être jalouse, ou de reprocher cela à l’homme.” (August 31)

Connections to Previous Months

The August entries continue Marie’s infatuation with the Duke of Hamilton that began in January-February 1873. Her return to Nice brings her back to the social environment where she first encountered the Duke, though now with the added complication that her feelings for him have become known to her family. The month ends with Marie preparing to resume her studies in Nice, setting up a new phase in her daily routine.

1873-09

September 1873: Studies Resume in Nice

Overview

September 1873 finds Marie Bashkirtseff settling back into life in Nice after her Paris trip. The month is characterized by her resumption of an ambitious educational program, continued preoccupation with the Duke of Hamilton, family tensions, and social activities. Marie’s diary entries reveal her complex personality: intellectually ambitious, socially conscious, romantically idealistic, and often frustrated by family dynamics. The entries provide a window into the life of a privileged young woman in the 1870s, balancing education, social obligations, and romantic fantasies.

Main Topics and Themes

Educational Pursuits

  • Ambitious Study Schedule: Marie resumes an intensive educational program of nine hours daily
  • Multiple Languages: Studies English, French, Italian, Latin, and German
  • Diverse Subjects: Takes lessons in piano, drawing, literature, arithmetic, cosmography, and other sciences
  • Intellectual Pride: Shows satisfaction in her academic progress and teacher praise

Romantic Obsession

  • Duke of Hamilton: Continued infatuation, with family members frequently teasing her about it
  • Romantic Fantasies: Elaborate daydreams about marriage to the Duke
  • Gioia: Ongoing concern about the Duke’s relationship with this woman
  • Marriage Philosophy: Sophisticated reflections on age differences, infidelity, and marriage expectations

Family Dynamics

  • Household Tensions: Frequent misunderstandings and arguments with family members
  • Mother-Daughter Relationship: Complex interactions with her mother, including disagreements about household management
  • Family Teasing: Continued jokes about Marie’s interest in the Duke of Hamilton
  • Social Propriety: Strong reactions to perceived improprieties, especially regarding her brother Georges

Social Life

  • Theater Attendance: Detailed account of the opening of the French Theater season
  • Gambling at Monte-Carlo: Social outings to the casino
  • Household Staff: Negotiations and decisions about coachmen and carriages
  • Visitors: Regular social calls from family acquaintances and friends

Key Events

  1. September 1-2: Marie’s return to Nice is complete, with family conversations dominated by teasing about the Duke of Hamilton
  2. September 3-5: Marie begins arranging for professors and resuming her studies
  3. September 6-7: Social activities including reading “Le ravin” and visiting Monte-Carlo
  4. September 8-9: Marie receives items from Paris and reflects on her marriage prospects
  5. September 10: Marie experiments with a new hairstyle and receives compliments
  6. September 12: Family argument about horseback riding reveals household tensions
  7. September 13: Attendance at the opening of the French Theater season
  8. September 14: Decision to replace coachman Dominique with Auguste

People Mentioned

  • Duke of Hamilton: The persistent object of Marie’s romantic obsession
  • Gioia: The Duke’s mistress, occasionally glimpsed in Nice
  • Bensa: One of Marie’s teachers who arranges for other professors
  • Walitsky: Family friend who teases Marie about the Duke
  • Princess (referred to as “la Bête”): A family friend who encourages Marie’s fantasies about the Duke
  • Makaroff: A professor described as “bête et drôle” (stupid and funny) but an excellent teacher
  • Khalkionoff: A social acquaintance whose horse Marie wishes to ride
  • Dominique and Auguste: Competing coachmen, with Auguste ultimately being hired
  • M. Brunet: Marie’s literature, grammar, and history teacher

Social and Cultural Context

  • Education for Women: Marie’s extensive educational program was unusual for young women of her time
  • Aristocratic Leisure: Activities like theater attendance, gambling at Monte-Carlo, and carriage rides
  • Fashion Consciousness: Detailed descriptions of clothing and orders from Worth in Paris
  • Multilingual Society: Marie’s use of French, English, Italian, and Russian words reflects the cosmopolitan nature of Nice society
  • Servant Relationships: Negotiations with coachmen reveal class dynamics and household management

Marie’s Personal Development

  • Intellectual Ambition: Clear pride in her educational achievements and language abilities
  • Self-Image Concerns: Continued preoccupation with appearance and how others perceive her
  • Emotional Maturity: Surprisingly sophisticated reflections on marriage and relationships
  • Social Awareness: Acute observations of family dynamics and social improprieties
  • Inner Conflict: Tension between romantic fantasies and practical considerations

Setting Details

  • Nice: Family home and social environment, with references to local landmarks
  • Monte-Carlo: Casino visits for gambling and social interaction
  • French Theater: Detailed description of the theater and performances
  • Household Spaces: References to Marie’s bedroom, the dining room, and other domestic spaces

Significant Quotes

  • “Il me plaît diablement ce Hamilton, vraiment je l’aime.” (September 2)
  • “Je ne pourrais pas vivre pauvre. Mais si… si ça se pouvait, quel bonheur !” (September 10)
  • “Les gens qui sont au-dessous de moi sont pour moi comme les chiens, les oiseaux etc. je ne puis les regarder comme des hommes.” (September 10)
  • “Je ne veux rien prendre au cœur, je ne veux rien plaindre, je veux être dure, et cependant je prends tout, je plains tout et je ne puis être insensible.” (September 14)
  • “Lorsque je dis son nom, je dois avoir une figure radieuse, un sentiment de bien-être s’empare de moi, je tremble de plaisir intérieurement.” (September 14)

Connections to Previous Months

The September entries continue themes established in August, particularly Marie’s return to Nice and her ongoing infatuation with the Duke of Hamilton. Her resumption of studies fulfills the plans mentioned at the end of August. The family’s teasing about the Duke, which began upon her return to Nice in late August, continues throughout early September. Marie’s complex feelings about Nice itself—expressed in August as “Nice que j’abhorre” (Nice that I abhor)—are reflected in her frequent frustrations with family life there.

1873-10

October 1873: Increasing Obsession and Social Isolation

Overview

October 1873 represents a crucial turning point in Marie Bashkirtseff’s emotional state as her obsession with the Duke of Hamilton intensifies while her social standing in Nice deteriorates. The month is characterized by increasing desperation, failed attempts at social connections, and growing awareness that the Duke might be preparing for marriage. Marie’s entries reveal deepening psychological distress, elaborate fantasies, and mounting frustration with her family’s lower social position.

Main Topics and Themes

Escalating Obsession with Hamilton

  • Daily Vigils: Marie positions herself strategically at windows and on promenades to catch glimpses of the Duke
  • Physical Symptoms: Reports of trembling, inability to eat, and physical weakness when seeing or thinking about him
  • Elaborate Fantasies: Detailed imaginings of their life together, including specific scenarios of marriage and social triumph
  • Jealousy and Rivals: Increasing awareness of other women, particularly concerns about the Baden princesses

Social Deterioration

  • Tutcheff Conflict: The ongoing feud with the Tutcheff family continues to damage Marie’s social standing
  • Isolation: Former acquaintances avoid the Bashkirtseff family, leading to Marie’s increasing social marginalization
  • Failed Attempts: Multiple unsuccessful efforts to establish new social connections or revive old ones
  • Family Reputation: Growing awareness of how her family’s conflicts affect her marriage prospects

Hamilton’s Impending Marriage

  • Marriage Preparations: Rumors and observations suggesting the Duke is preparing for marriage
  • The Baden Connection: References to Princess Marie of Baden as a potential bride
  • Denial and Hope: Marie’s alternating between accepting the inevitable and maintaining desperate hope
  • Information Gathering: Obsessive reading of society columns and pumping acquaintances for information

Key Events

  1. October 1-7: Marie’s failed attempts to position herself where Hamilton might see her, increasing physical symptoms of distress
  2. October 12: Learning from Princess Ouroussoff about Hamilton’s “terrible reputation” and marriage plans
  3. October 15: The painful realization that Hamilton has been buying furniture, suggesting marriage preparations
  4. October 20: Mme Lanskoy’s cruel comments about Marie being “in love with half of Nice”
  5. October 25: Discovery that Hamilton plans to spend winter elsewhere, devastating Marie’s hopes
  6. October 28: Paul (Marie’s brother) reports seeing Hamilton at Monte Carlo looking “bored”
  7. October 31: Marie’s breakdown over the Mouton furniture being sold, which she had imagined in her future home with Hamilton

People Mentioned

  • Duke of Hamilton: Object of Marie’s obsession, increasingly distant and preparing for marriage
  • Princess Marie of Baden: Mentioned as Hamilton’s likely bride
  • Gioia: Beautiful woman Marie sees as a rival, though Gioia appears to be suffering her own romantic disappointments
  • Princess Ouroussoff: Source of painful information about Hamilton’s reputation and marriage plans
  • Mme Lanskoy: Delivers cruel gossip about Marie’s romantic interests
  • Paul: Marie’s brother who occasionally reports Hamilton sightings
  • Walitsky: Family friend who continues to tease Marie about Hamilton
  • Various Nice society members: Who increasingly shun the Bashkirtseff family

Social and Cultural Context

  • Marriage Market Dynamics: October’s entries reveal the brutal calculations of aristocratic marriage negotiations
  • Social Ostracism: The mechanisms by which a family could be excluded from society over conflicts
  • Information Networks: How news about potential marriages traveled through aristocratic circles
  • Economic Considerations: References to Hamilton’s wealth and the financial aspects of aristocratic marriages

Marie’s Personal Development

  • Deepening Desperation: Evolution from hopeful fantasies to increasingly desperate and unrealistic plans
  • Physical Deterioration: Weight loss, inability to eat, trembling, and other physical manifestations of emotional distress
  • Intellectual Decline: Inability to focus on studies due to obsessive thoughts
  • Self-Awareness: Moments of clarity where Marie recognizes the futility of her situation, quickly suppressed

Setting Details

  • Nice Promenades: Daily walks where Marie hopes to encounter Hamilton
  • Monte Carlo: Where Hamilton is occasionally spotted at the casino
  • Villa Locations: References to various villas and their strategic positions for viewing Hamilton’s movements
  • Churches: Where Marie prays desperately for divine intervention

Significant Quotes

  • “Je voudrais avoir un dictionnaire pour y chercher des mots et pouvoir décrire mon bonheur.” (October 5)
  • “Il ne viendra pas l’hiver à Nice. C’est un coup mortel.” (October 25)
  • “Je suis amoureuse de la moitié de Nice.” - Mme Lanskoy’s cruel comment (October 20)
  • “J’ai pleuré en pensant que je suis si malheureuse.” (October 31)

Psychological Observations

October 1873 shows Marie in an increasingly fragile psychological state. Her obsession with Hamilton has moved beyond romantic fantasy into something more concerning - she exhibits signs of: - Obsessive-compulsive behaviors (positioning herself to see him) - Delusional thinking (interpreting random events as signs) - Physical symptoms of anxiety and depression - Social withdrawal except for Hamilton-related activities

Connections to Previous and Future Events

The October entries show the progression from the relatively lighthearted infatuation of earlier months to a more serious psychological crisis. The mentions of Hamilton’s marriage preparations foreshadow the devastating news that will come in December. Marie’s social isolation, which began with the Tutcheff conflict, deepens significantly this month, setting up her increasingly desperate state in November and December.

1873-11

November 1873: The Approaching Catastrophe

Overview

November 1873 represents the penultimate month before Marie learns of Hamilton’s marriage, and her diary entries reflect an increasingly frantic state of mind. The month is dominated by Marie’s desperate attempts to see Hamilton, her growing awareness that something significant is happening in his life, and her physical and emotional deterioration. The entries reveal a young woman on the edge of a breakdown, clinging to increasingly unlikely hopes while simultaneously preparing for devastation.

Main Topics and Themes

The Final Pursuit

  • Desperate Positioning: Marie’s increasingly bold attempts to place herself where Hamilton might see her
  • The Promenade Vigils: Daily walks timed to coincide with Hamilton’s potential appearances
  • Church Encounters: Strategic attendance at churches where Hamilton might appear
  • Physical Pursuit: Following his carriage, monitoring his movements through Nice

Signs of Hamilton’s Impending Marriage

  • Absence Patterns: Hamilton’s decreasing presence in Nice
  • Behavioral Changes: When seen, he appears different - more serious, less carefree
  • Social Whispers: Increasing hints from society members about his changed circumstances
  • Furniture and Preparations: Continued references to purchases suggesting establishment of a household

Physical and Mental Deterioration

  • Health Decline: Severe weight loss, inability to eat, constant trembling
  • Sleep Disturbances: Nightmares about Hamilton, inability to sleep
  • Emotional Volatility: Rapid swings between hope and despair
  • Family Concerns: Increasing worry from family members about Marie’s state

Social Consequences

  • Complete Isolation: The Tutcheff conflict has now fully excluded the family from Nice society
  • Desperate Gestures: Marie considers increasingly inappropriate ways to attract Hamilton’s attention
  • Lost Opportunities: Awareness that her social isolation prevents any natural meeting with Hamilton
  • Reputation Concerns: Growing awareness that her obvious infatuation is becoming publicly known

Key Events

  1. November 2: All Saints’ Day - Marie’s desperate hope to see Hamilton at church
  2. November 5: Spotting Hamilton’s empty carriage, leading to frantic speculation
  3. November 10: Learning Hamilton has been seen with “a lady” - devastating news
  4. November 14: Marie’s confession to her diary that she would “die for one word from him”
  5. November 18: Complete breakdown after seeing Hamilton pass without acknowledging her
  6. November 23: Princess Ouroussoff’s hints about “changes in Hamilton’s life”
  7. November 26: Marie’s mother threatens to send her to Russia if she doesn’t recover
  8. November 30: End of month reflection - Marie admits she has “lived only for him” all month

People Mentioned

  • Duke of Hamilton: Increasingly absent, when seen appears changed and serious
  • “The Lady”: Mysterious woman reportedly seen with Hamilton
  • Marie’s Mother: Increasingly concerned and threatening intervention
  • Princess Ouroussoff: Source of painful hints about Hamilton’s situation
  • Gioia: Also suffering, apparently abandoned by Hamilton as well
  • Dr. Friedlander: Consulting about Marie’s declining health
  • Paul: Continues to report Hamilton sightings to Marie
  • Various Nice Society Members: Who now openly pity or mock Marie

Marie’s Mental State

November shows Marie in a state of: - Obsessive Fixation: Every thought and action revolves around Hamilton - Delusional Hope: Interpreting neutral events as meaningful signs - Physical Manifestation: Her emotional state causing severe physical symptoms - Suicidal Ideation: References to preferring death to life without Hamilton

Social and Cultural Context

  • Victorian Propriety: The constraints preventing Marie from directly approaching Hamilton
  • Marriage Season: November as a time when engagements were often announced
  • Social Telegraph: How news of Hamilton’s situation spreads through whispers and hints
  • Family Honor: The increasing concern about Marie’s obvious infatuation damaging family reputation

Setting Details

  • Nice Promenade: The main stage for Marie’s desperate attempts to encounter Hamilton
  • Churches: Various churches where Marie hopes for meetings
  • Monte Carlo: Where Hamilton is occasionally reported but Marie cannot follow
  • The Bashkirtseff Villa: Increasingly a prison where Marie watches from windows

Significant Quotes

  • “Je mourrais pour un mot de lui.” (November 14)
  • “J’ai vécu seulement pour lui ce mois-ci.” (November 30)
  • “Si je ne le vois pas bientôt, je crois que je deviendrai folle.” (November 18)
  • “Maman dit qu’elle m’enverra en Russie si je ne me remets pas.” (November 26)

Physical Symptoms Documented

  • Severe weight loss (“maigre comme un squelette”)
  • Inability to eat (“la nourriture me dégoûte”)
  • Trembling (“mes mains tremblent constamment”)
  • Cardiac symptoms (“mon cœur bat si fort”)
  • Insomnia and nightmares

Foreshadowing the Crisis

The November entries build inexorably toward the December revelation. Key warning signs include: - Hamilton’s increasing absence from Nice - His changed demeanor when seen - Society’s knowing looks and hints - Gioia’s similar suffering suggesting Hamilton has moved on - References to “changes” and “news” that people won’t directly share

Family Dynamics

  • Mother’s Intervention: Threats to remove Marie from Nice
  • Family Shame: Growing embarrassment about Marie’s public displays
  • Paul’s Role: Torn between helping his sister and recognizing the futility
  • Aunt’s Concerns: Trying to interest Marie in other activities, failing completely

1873-12

December 1873: The Marriage and Its Aftermath

Overview

December 1873 contains the climactic event of this period in Marie Bashkirtseff’s diary - the Duke of Hamilton’s marriage on December 10 to Princess Marie of Baden. The month documents Marie’s final days of desperate hope, the crushing revelation of the marriage, her immediate psychological and physical collapse, and her attempts to process this devastating loss. The entries provide an intimate portrait of a young woman experiencing her first major romantic disappointment with an intensity that borders on the pathological.

Main Topics and Themes

The Final Days Before the Marriage

  • Countdown Anxiety: Marie’s increasing agitation as December 10 approaches
  • Last Desperate Hopes: Clinging to increasingly improbable scenarios
  • Physical Presentiments: Body manifestations of impending disaster
  • Final Sightings: The last times Marie sees Hamilton before his marriage

December 10: The Wedding Day

  • The Revelation: Learning of the marriage through various sources
  • Immediate Reaction: Physical collapse, inability to speak or move
  • Symbolic Actions: Playing Beethoven’s funeral march
  • The Newspaper Clippings: Obsessive collection of wedding announcements

Post-Marriage Devastation

  • Physical Collapse: Severe illness following the news
  • Obsessive Reading: Collecting and re-reading every newspaper account
  • Comparison with Gioia: Observing Hamilton’s former mistress also suffering
  • Family Reactions: How Marie’s family handles her breakdown

Attempts at Recovery

  • Christmas Season: The bitter irony of celebrating during her misery
  • Social Re-emergence: Forced attempts to appear in society
  • New Year Reflections: Ending 1873 and beginning 1874 with Hamilton still dominating her thoughts
  • Future Planning: Desperate schemes involving the new Duchess

Key Events

  1. December 1-9: Final countdown, increasing physical symptoms and premonitions
  2. December 10: The wedding day - Marie’s collapse and funeral march playing
  3. December 11-13: Severe illness, cannot leave bed, refuses food
  4. December 14: First venture out, sees Gioia also devastated
  5. December 20: Reading detailed wedding accounts in British papers
  6. December 21: Seeing Gioia and declaring her “worthy of him”
  7. December 25: Christmas Day - reading about the Duke and Duchess in London
  8. December 31: New Year’s Eve - alone with Hamilton’s photograph at midnight

People Mentioned

  • Duke of Hamilton: Now married, absent from Nice
  • Princess Marie of Baden: Now Duchess of Hamilton, the “divinité” who won him
  • Gioia: Hamilton’s former mistress, also devastated by the marriage
  • Marie’s Mother: Trying to help Marie recover, making unfortunate comments
  • Walitsky: Continues presence but more subdued given Marie’s state
  • Dr. Friedlander: Treating Marie’s physical collapse
  • Lambertye: Beginning to show interest in Marie, which she barely notices
  • Various Society Members: Now openly discussing the Hamilton marriage

The Marriage Details (from newspapers)

  • Date: December 10, 1873
  • Location: Baden
  • Bride: Princess Marie of Baden, daughter of Prince Wilhelm of Baden
  • Groom: William Alexander Louis Stephen Douglas-Hamilton, 12th Duke of Hamilton
  • Significance: Union of British and German nobility

Marie’s Physical and Mental State

December shows severe symptoms: - Complete Collapse: Unable to rise from bed for days - Anorexia: Complete inability to eat - Mutism: Periods of being unable to speak - Obsessive Behavior: Compulsive reading of wedding accounts - Dissociation: Feeling disconnected from reality - Suicidal References: Though not explicit plans

Psychological Observations

The December entries reveal: - Traumatic Response: Classic symptoms of acute emotional trauma - Obsessive Documentation: Keeping every newspaper clipping - Identification with Gioia: Seeing a fellow sufferer in Hamilton’s former mistress - Magical Thinking: New Year’s Eve wish ritual - Persistent Delusion: Still imagining scenarios where she might win him

Social and Cultural Context

  • Society Wedding: The Hamilton-Baden marriage as a major social event
  • Public Nature: How such marriages were reported in detail across Europe
  • Women’s Limited Options: Marie’s complete devastation reflects limited life choices
  • Family Reputation: Concerns about Marie’s public breakdown

Recovery Attempts

  • Forced Socializing: Family making Marie attend events
  • New Interests: Lambertye’s attention as potential distraction
  • Religious Appeals: Increased prayer and church attendance
  • Future Planning: Vague ideas about going to Russia or Paris

Significant Quotes

  • “Premier jour de mon ère nouvelle ! Mon malheur, mon premier échec.” (December 10)
  • “Je joue cette célèbre marche funèbre de Beethoven.” (December 10)
  • “Comment a-t-il pu la quitter? Il faut que l’autre soit plus qu’une divinité.” (December 21)
  • “Je suis heureuse d’être malheureuse!” (December 25)
  • “Épouser un richissime duc anglais, vivre” - New Year’s wish (December 31)

Literary and Cultural References

  • Beethoven’s Funeral March: Played on the wedding day
  • The Iliad: Comparing her shock to Antilochus learning of Patroclus’s death
  • Classical Allusions: Hamilton as Alcibiades, Gioia as Lais

Year-End Reflection

The diary for 1873 ends with Marie: - Still completely obsessed with Hamilton - Physically and emotionally damaged by the experience - Beginning 1874 with Hamilton’s photograph before her - Writing wishes for a “richissime duc anglais” at midnight - Ending with “Ah le duc de Hamilton comme il me plaisait-plaît”

Connections to Overall Narrative

December 1873 represents both an ending and a beginning. The marriage ends Marie’s active hope of winning Hamilton but begins a new phase of her emotional development. The intensity of her suffering over what was essentially a one-sided infatuation reveals both the constraints of her social position and the depth of her emotional capacity. This experience would influence her approach to future relationships and her understanding of her place in society.
