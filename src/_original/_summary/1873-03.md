# March 1873 - Monthly Summary

## Overview
The March 1873 entries (1st-12th) conclude Marie Bashkirtseff's second notebook (Carnet NÂ° 2). This period marks a significant transition in Marie's life as she learns that the Duke of Hamilton has departed Nice, forcing her to reconsider her romantic aspirations. The month also features a pivotal moment when Marie discovers her singing talent might provide a path to fame and the Duke's attention.

## Key Themes

### The Departure of the Duke
Early in March, Marie learns that the Duke of Hamilton's yacht has left Nice eight days earlier, meaning he has departed without her ever having been properly introduced to him. This news devastates her:
- She expresses rage and despair at being "ignored" by him
- Laments having no purpose for her promenades and social appearances
- Imagines him in Paris, planning to somehow see him there
- Compares her feelings to those for someone who has died

### Singing as a Path to Glory
A transformative moment occurs on March 9 when Marie sings for the Howard children and receives enthusiastic praise. This experience leads to an epiphany:
- She realizes her voice could make her a celebrated singer (cantatrice)
- Envisions a career that would bring her fame, admiration, and social power
- Believes this path could ultimately win the Duke's attention and love
- Sees this as divinely inspired - God showing her how to achieve her desires
- Reconciles her aristocratic status with a performing career by emphasizing her noble birth

### Continued Ambivalence Toward Boreel
Marie's feelings about Boreel remain complicated:
- She criticizes him for imitating the Duke's lifestyle without having the means
- Expresses disappointment at his visits to Monaco and rumors of his behavior there
- Admits to missing seeing him on the promenade
- Notes with satisfaction when he looks at her admiringly
- Strategizes about appearing indifferent to increase his interest

### Family Crisis
On March 11, Marie's mother suffers what appears to be a nervous attack:
- The family fears for her life momentarily
- Marie describes this as "the strongest emotion I have ever experienced"
- Multiple doctors are called
- By the following day, her mother is improving but still bedridden
- The incident reveals Marie's genuine attachment to her mother despite her self-absorption

### Social Activities
Marie continues her regular social routine:
- Daily promenades (on foot, in carriages, or occasionally on horseback)
- Theater and opera attendance (including Verdi's "La Forza del Destino" and "Lucia di Lammermoor")
- Family's Monday reception day
- Visits to and from acquaintances
- Photography session at Shier's studio

## Key People

- **Duke of Hamilton** - The primary object of Marie's romantic obsession, who has now left Nice
- **Alfred Boreel** - Young man who continues to interest Marie despite her criticisms
- **Gioia** - Woman associated with the Duke, presumed to have left Nice with him
- **The Howard family** - Social connections whose children's admiration of Marie's singing inspires her career ambitions
- **Mlle Collignon** - Marie's teacher, who earns praise for her helpfulness during the mother's health crisis
- **Maman** (Marie's mother) - Suffers a health crisis that briefly shifts Marie's focus from romantic concerns
- **Count Gabrielli** - Social acquaintance who inadvertently delivers the news of the Duke's departure

## Key Places

- **Paris** - Where Marie believes the Duke has gone, and where she hopes to see him at the Bois de Boulogne
- **Monaco** - Described as a "cursed place" and "awful abyss" that corrupts young men like Boreel
- **The Italian and French Theaters** - Venues for performances Marie attends
- **The Howard residence** - Where Marie's singing talent is recognized and celebrated

## Marie's Inner Life

The March entries reveal significant aspects of Marie's psychology:
- Her tendency to dramatize emotions while simultaneously analyzing them
- Her strategic thinking about social and romantic situations
- Her ability to quickly pivot from disappointment to new ambitions
- Her religious framework for understanding events and desires
- Her self-awareness about her appearance and effect on others
- Her class consciousness and social ambitions
- Her later self-criticism (evident in marginal notes added in 1874)

## Significant Developments

1. **The Duke's Departure** - Forces Marie to confront the unlikelihood of her romantic fantasy
2. **Discovery of Vocal Talent** - Provides a new direction for her ambitions
3. **Mother's Health Crisis** - Momentarily shifts Marie's focus from romantic concerns to family
4. **Career Ambitions** - Marie begins to envision herself as a celebrated singer while maintaining her social status

## Historical Context

These entries provide valuable insights into:
- The conclusion of the winter social season in Nice
- The importance of proper introductions and social protocols in aristocratic circles
- The tension between aristocratic values and artistic careers for women
- The perception of Monaco as a place of moral corruption
- The cultural significance of opera and theater in 1870s European society
