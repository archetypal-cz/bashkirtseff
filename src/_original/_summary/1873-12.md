# December 1873: The Marriage and Its Aftermath

## Overview

December 1873 contains the climactic event of this period in Marie Bashkirtseff's diary - the Duke of Hamilton's marriage on December 10 to Princess Marie of Baden. The month documents Marie's final days of desperate hope, the crushing revelation of the marriage, her immediate psychological and physical collapse, and her attempts to process this devastating loss. The entries provide an intimate portrait of a young woman experiencing her first major romantic disappointment with an intensity that borders on the pathological.

## Main Topics and Themes

### The Final Days Before the Marriage
— **Countdown Anxiety**: Marie's increasing agitation as December 10 approaches
— **Last Desperate Hopes**: Clinging to increasingly improbable scenarios
— **Physical Presentiments**: Body manifestations of impending disaster
— **Final Sightings**: The last times Marie sees Hamilton before his marriage

### December 10: The Wedding Day
— **The Revelation**: Learning of the marriage through various sources
— **Immediate Reaction**: Physical collapse, inability to speak or move
— **Symbolic Actions**: Playing Beethoven's funeral march
— **The Newspaper Clippings**: Obsessive collection of wedding announcements

### Post-Marriage Devastation
— **Physical Collapse**: Severe illness following the news
— **Obsessive Reading**: Collecting and re-reading every newspaper account
— **Comparison with Gioia**: Observing Hamilton's former mistress also suffering
— **Family Reactions**: How Marie's family handles her breakdown

### Attempts at Recovery
— **Christmas Season**: The bitter irony of celebrating during her misery
— **Social Re-emergence**: Forced attempts to appear in society
— **New Year Reflections**: Ending 1873 and beginning 1874 with Hamilton still dominating her thoughts
— **Future Planning**: Desperate schemes involving the new Duchess

## Key Events

1. **December 1-9**: Final countdown, increasing physical symptoms and premonitions
2. **December 10**: The wedding day - Marie's collapse and funeral march playing
3. **December 11-13**: Severe illness, cannot leave bed, refuses food
4. **December 14**: First venture out, sees Gioia also devastated
5. **December 20**: Reading detailed wedding accounts in British papers
6. **December 21**: Seeing Gioia and declaring her "worthy of him"
7. **December 25**: Christmas Day - reading about the Duke and Duchess in London
8. **December 31**: New Year's Eve - alone with Hamilton's photograph at midnight

## People Mentioned

— **Duke of Hamilton**: Now married, absent from Nice
— **Princess Marie of Baden**: Now Duchess of Hamilton, the "divinité" who won him
— **Gioia**: Hamilton's former mistress, also devastated by the marriage
— **Marie's Mother**: Trying to help Marie recover, making unfortunate comments
— **Walitsky**: Continues presence but more subdued given Marie's state
— **Dr. Friedlander**: Treating Marie's physical collapse
— **Lambertye**: Beginning to show interest in Marie, which she barely notices
— **Various Society Members**: Now openly discussing the Hamilton marriage

## The Marriage Details (from newspapers)

— **Date**: December 10, 1873
— **Location**: Baden
— **Bride**: Princess Marie of Baden, daughter of Prince Wilhelm of Baden
— **Groom**: William Alexander Louis Stephen Douglas-Hamilton, 12th Duke of Hamilton
— **Significance**: Union of British and German nobility

## Marie's Physical and Mental State

December shows severe symptoms:
— **Complete Collapse**: Unable to rise from bed for days
— **Anorexia**: Complete inability to eat
— **Mutism**: Periods of being unable to speak
— **Obsessive Behavior**: Compulsive reading of wedding accounts
— **Dissociation**: Feeling disconnected from reality
— **Suicidal References**: Though not explicit plans

## Psychological Observations

The December entries reveal:
— **Traumatic Response**: Classic symptoms of acute emotional trauma
— **Obsessive Documentation**: Keeping every newspaper clipping
— **Identification with Gioia**: Seeing a fellow sufferer in Hamilton's former mistress
— **Magical Thinking**: New Year's Eve wish ritual
— **Persistent Delusion**: Still imagining scenarios where she might win him

## Social and Cultural Context

— **Society Wedding**: The Hamilton-Baden marriage as a major social event
— **Public Nature**: How such marriages were reported in detail across Europe
— **Women's Limited Options**: Marie's complete devastation reflects limited life choices
— **Family Reputation**: Concerns about Marie's public breakdown

## Recovery Attempts

— **Forced Socializing**: Family making Marie attend events
— **New Interests**: Lambertye's attention as potential distraction
— **Religious Appeals**: Increased prayer and church attendance
— **Future Planning**: Vague ideas about going to Russia or Paris

## Significant Quotes

— "Premier jour de mon ère nouvelle ! Mon malheur, mon premier échec." (December 10)
— "Je joue cette célèbre marche funèbre de Beethoven." (December 10)
— "Comment a-t-il pu la quitter? Il faut que l'autre soit plus qu'une divinité." (December 21)
— "Je suis heureuse d'être malheureuse!" (December 25)
— "Épouser un richissime duc anglais, vivre" - New Year's wish (December 31)

## Literary and Cultural References

— **Beethoven's Funeral March**: Played on the wedding day
— **The Iliad**: Comparing her shock to Antilochus learning of Patroclus's death
— **Classical Allusions**: Hamilton as Alcibiades, Gioia as Lais

## Year-End Reflection

The diary for 1873 ends with Marie:
— Still completely obsessed with Hamilton
— Physically and emotionally damaged by the experience
— Beginning 1874 with Hamilton's photograph before her
— Writing wishes for a "richissime duc anglais" at midnight
— Ending with "Ah le duc de Hamilton comme il me plaisait-plaît"

## Connections to Overall Narrative

December 1873 represents both an ending and a beginning. The marriage ends Marie's active hope of winning Hamilton but begins a new phase of her emotional development. The intensity of her suffering over what was essentially a one-sided infatuation reveals both the constraints of her social position and the depth of her emotional capacity. This experience would influence her approach to future relationships and her understanding of her place in society.
